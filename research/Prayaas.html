PRAYAAS

My PRAYAAS project studied why students in Punjab were not choosing science streams at the same rate as students nationally. I looked at the academic, social, and systemic factors behind low science uptake, especially in Classes 11 and 12.

How it started

This project grew out of my work with the Sustainability Leaders Program . While engaging with students across Punjab, I noticed that many students were interested in real-world problems, but relatively few were choosing science streams in senior secondary school.

That made me curious. If science is connected to careers, technology, innovation, medicine, climate, and policy, why were so many students not choosing it? I wanted to understand whether the issue was interest, access, school resources, family expectations, confidence, awareness, or something else entirely.

The problem

Punjab had a noticeably low science-stream enrollment rate. In Classes 11 and 12, only about 21% of students were opting for science. In government schools, the number was even lower, around 12%, compared with a national average of about 33%.

I wanted to study this as more than just a statistic. Low science enrollment affects educational equity, future career options, skilled workforce development, and the ability of students to participate in technology-driven fields.

What I studied

The project focused on understanding the causes behind low science uptake in Punjab. I looked at differences between government and private schools, gender-based disparities, and the role of school resources, student confidence, family awareness, and career guidance.

The goal was not just to describe the problem, but to collect enough evidence to suggest practical policy interventions that could help reverse the trend.

Research design

I used both primary and secondary sources. The primary research included questionnaires, interviews, and focus group discussions with students, teachers, parents, and other stakeholders. The secondary research included data from educational organizations and existing enrollment information.

I used stratified random sampling so that the study could capture differences across school types, regions, and student groups. I also separated parts of the data by gender to examine whether boys and girls were facing different barriers to choosing science.

The final study involved surveys and stakeholder input from 6,000+ students and faculty across 244 schools. I analyzed the responses using both quantitative and qualitative methods to understand patterns in the data.

What I found meaningful

The most important part of the project was realizing that low science uptake was not caused by one single factor. It was connected to school infrastructure, perceived difficulty, access to labs and teachers, awareness of career paths, confidence, family expectations, and whether students could actually imagine themselves succeeding in science.

I also saw how education data can become a starting point for policy. A problem that initially looked like a student-choice issue was also about systems, resources, and opportunity.

Where it went

This project was selected under NCERT PRAYAAS. I was among the top 20 out of 1000+ youth researchers and received a one-year national research grant.

I presented the findings to education stakeholders and used the project to think more seriously about how evidence-based research can help address educational equity gaps.

What I took from it

PRAYAAS changed how I think about research. It taught me that data is not just numbers on a spreadsheet. Behind every response is a student making decisions within a larger system of schools, families, expectations, and opportunities.

It also made me more interested in work that combines research, policy, and social impact. A good solution has to understand the people affected by the problem before trying to fix it.
