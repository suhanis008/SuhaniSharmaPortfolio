research / education equity

NCERT PRAYAAS

A research project studying why science-stream uptake remains low among senior secondary students in Punjab, and what schools, families, and policymakers can do to improve student access, awareness, and confidence.

How it started

This project grew out of my work with the Sustainability Leaders Program. While engaging with students across Punjab, I noticed that many students were interested in real-world problems, but relatively few were choosing science streams in senior secondary school.

That made me curious. If science connects to careers, technology, innovation, medicine, climate, and policy, why were so many students not choosing it? I wanted to understand whether the issue was interest, access, school resources, family expectations, confidence, awareness, or something else entirely.

The problem

Punjab had a noticeably low science-stream enrollment rate. In Classes 11 and 12, only about 21% of students were opting for science. In government schools, the number was even lower, around 12%, compared with a national average of about 33%.

I wanted to study this as more than just a statistic. Low science enrollment affects educational equity, future career options, skilled workforce development, and the ability of students to participate in technology-driven fields.

What I studied

The project focused on understanding the causes behind low science uptake in Punjab. I looked at differences between government and private schools, gender-based disparities, school resources, student confidence, family awareness, and career guidance.

The goal was not just to describe the problem, but to collect enough evidence to suggest practical policy interventions that could help reverse the trend.

Research design

I used both primary and secondary sources. The primary research included field visits and conversations with students, teachers, and school stakeholders. I also studied student perspectives to understand how they think about science, careers, difficulty level, family expectations, and future opportunities.

The secondary research involved reviewing available data, reports, and policy material related to science education, school resources, and enrollment patterns.

What I found

A major issue was not simply lack of interest. Many students were curious about science, but did not always see a clear path from science subjects to future careers. For some students, science felt risky, expensive, or too difficult without the right support.

I also found that access to labs, teachers, counseling, and exposure to real science careers played an important role. Students who had more guidance were more likely to see science as possible and useful.

Possible interventions

The project suggested that improving science uptake would require more than asking students to choose science. Schools need stronger career guidance, hands-on exposure, mentoring, better science infrastructure, and communication with families about what science can lead to.

I was especially interested in interventions that are practical at the school level, such as student workshops, teacher support, science career sessions, peer role models, and targeted outreach in government schools.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how education choices are shaped by much more than personal preference. A student’s decision depends on confidence, family context, school resources, information, and the way future opportunities are presented to them.

It also made me more interested in the connection between education, policy, and equity. Science should not feel like an option only for students who already have the right resources. It should feel accessible to students who are curious and willing to try.
