CNC Milling

This project was an introduction to CNC milling and post-processing. I designed and milled wooden jewelry trays, then experimented with sanding, finishing, vacuum forming, and plaster casting.

Project overview

For this project, I wanted to make something useful and personal. I started with a wooden jewelry tray as a birthday present for my friend, then made a second, more complex version featuring a Harvard “H” in the center.

The project helped me understand the full CNC workflow: designing in Fusion 360, preparing the toolpaths, cutting on the ShopBot, sanding the wood, finishing the surface, and later using the milled part to create a mold for plaster casting.

First jewelry tray

I began by designing a simple tray in Fusion 360. The shape was rounded and shallow, so it could hold small objects like jewelry without being too difficult to cut.

I then used Aspire and the ShopBot to cut the design on the CNC machine. The first cut came out rough, but the shape was visible. After sanding, the tray looked much smoother.

Second iteration: Harvard “H” tray

After getting more comfortable with the ShopBot, I wanted to try something more complex. I designed an irregular-shaped jewelry tray with a Harvard “H” in the center.

I had previously run into issues with the Join command in Aspire. For this version, with Bobby’s guidance, I created a full 3D model and projected it onto a plane to get a single connected sketch. I used pocket cuts so that the middle “H” would sit lower than the surrounding edges.

One section of the wood turned out to be hollow, so I filled it with wood filler and later reinforced it with wood glue. This was a good reminder that the material itself can introduce unexpected issues, even when the digital model and toolpath are correct.

Vacuum forming and casting

Once the tray was fully sanded, I used vacuum forming to create a mold. This let me turn the CNC-milled wooden tray into a form that could be used for casting.

Inspired by swirly paint designs I had seen in the lab, I experimented with adding blue paint to the plaster mixture. After mixing plaster powder and water in the correct ratio, I added a few drops of paint to create a marbled effect.

Final pieces

After removing the cast from the mold, I was really happy with how it turned out. The blue swirls gave the plaster version a soft marbled finish. I also applied shellac to the wooden tray to make it look more polished.

Reflection

I really enjoyed learning how to use the CNC machine. This project helped me see how digital modeling, machine setup, material behavior, sanding, finishing, molding, and casting all connect. It also made me want to keep experimenting with CNC milling in future projects.
